Urban Ecology and Immigration: The Heat Island Effect

A simple interactive website about how city design, green space, and environmental inequality affect heat exposure in immigrant communities.

Topic overview

Heat is not the same everywhere.

Urban heat islands form when pavement, dark roofs, traffic, and dense buildings absorb and re-radiate heat. Communities with fewer trees and more impervious surfaces can experience higher temperatures than nearby greener neighborhoods.

Research Question

How do temperature patterns in urban heat islands overlap with immigrant communities, green-space access, and respiratory health risks?

Why It Matters

Heat exposure can worsen asthma, dehydration, and air-quality problems. This topic connects climate science to real neighborhoods and the people who live there.

Existing research

What our research showed

Urban Form

Asphalt, concrete, and building density store heat during the day and release it at night. Tree canopy and parks cool neighborhoods through shade and evapotranspiration.

Environmental Justice

Some neighborhoods have fewer trees, older housing, and less cooling infrastructure. Immigrant communities can also face language barriers during heat warnings or public-health emergencies.

Health Impacts

Extreme heat and poor air quality can increase respiratory stress. Heat can also intensify ground-level ozone, which is connected to asthma and other breathing problems.

Conclusion and extensions

What this means

Heat islands are caused by materials like asphalt and concrete, but the effects are also connected to housing, work, language access, and healthcare. Solutions should focus on neighborhoods where heat exposure and vulnerability overlap.

Possible Solutions

Tree planting, reflective roofs, shade structures, cooling centers, multilingual emergency alerts, tenant protections, and traffic/pollution reduction can work together.

Contemporary Extension

Future versions could compare local neighborhoods, add live weather data, or map cooling resources against public transit access.
