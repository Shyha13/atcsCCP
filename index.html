Urban Ecology and Immigration: The Heat Island Effect

A simple interactive website about how city design, green space, and environmental inequality affect heat exposure in immigrant communities.

Topic overview

Heat is not the same everywhere.

Urban heat islands form when natural landscapes are replaced by roads, rooftops, and other infrastructure. Materials such as asphalt, concrete, and brick absorb solar energy during the day and slowly release it at night, while the loss of trees reduces shade and evapotranspiration.

Research Question

How do urban heat island temperatures, tree-canopy coverage, respiratory burden, and linguistic isolation compare across U.S. cities?

Why It Matters

Extreme heat can increase heat-related illness and respiratory problems. Communities with language barriers may also have less access to heat warnings and emergency information.

Existing research

What our research showed

Thermodynamics

Built surfaces hold solar energy as sensible heat. Vegetation uses some energy for evapotranspiration instead, which helps cool the surrounding air.

Environmental Justice

Research in New York City found that Hispanic and Latine neighborhoods often have more features that increase heat and fewer features that provide cooling.

Urban Realism

The House on Mango Street and other Chicano literature use crowded housing, concrete, heat, and small pieces of nature to show exclusion, confinement, and resilience.

Methodology

How urban heat can be measured

Air Temperature

Land monitors and mobile routes measure the heat people directly experience, but they only cover part of a city.

Surface Temperature

Landsat, MODIS, aircraft, and thermal sensors compare heat emitted by roads, rooftops, buildings, and vegetation across a larger area.

Latent Heat

Thermal cameras can compare planted and non-planted surfaces to show how evapotranspiration uses energy and lowers temperatures.

Conclusion and extensions

What this means

The data shows a general inverse relationship: cities with less tree canopy tend to have higher heat anomalies and greater respiratory burdens. Newark has 9% tree canopy, a +9.0°F anomaly, and an 8.4% respiratory burden, while Atlanta has 48% canopy, a +5.0°F anomaly, and a 5.2% respiratory burden.

Possible Solutions

Planting more trees is the main solution supported by our findings because shade and evapotranspiration cool urban surfaces. Reflective roofs and multilingual heat alerts can provide additional help.

Environmental Justice

New York and Newark combine high heat anomalies with 25% and 28% linguistic isolation. This supports targeted greening and accessible emergency information in the most vulnerable communities.
